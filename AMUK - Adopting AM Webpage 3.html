Assessing Needs & Feasibility

Guide

Begin by conducting a thorough assessment of your current manufacturing processes, identifying the pain points, inefficiencies, or limitations. Consider factors like production volumes, part complexity, customization needs, and lead times.

Understand if additive manufacturing (AM) could address specific challenges, such as reducing tooling costs, enabling part consolidation, reducing weight, or producing highly customized products. Also, assess whether AM can fit into your budget and timeline, keeping in mind that the investment in 3D printing technologies should align with your company's growth objectives and long-term goals.

Case Studies

Schunk, a manufacturer of customized grippers for production processes, has partnered with Belgian company Materialise to create an online platform leveraging additive manufacturing. This platform allows clients to customize standard gripper shapes digitally, reducing production time and resource use. The process, which takes about 20 minutes to complete online, enables grippers to be 3D printed and delivered within a week. This innovation significantly decreases the need for intensive engineering collaboration and provides Schunk with valuable data on customer preferences and demand patterns.

The Port of Rotterdam is exploring high-end additive manufacturing to gain a competitive edge in the fourth industrial revolution. With plans to establish an industrial print hub as part of its smart port strategy, the port aims to enable businesses to source high-quality industrial and maritime parts on demand, reducing costly downtimes. To lay the groundwork, the port has partnered with Innovation Quarter and RDM Makerspace to pilot a program identifying suitable parts for 3D printing. This initiative will build expertise, assess opportunities and challenges, gather business case data, and guide future steps toward creating a functional industrial printing hub.


Grants & Funding

The UK has established Catapult Centres, such as the High Value Manufacturing Catapult, to support businesses in adopting advanced manufacturing technologies, including additive manufacturing. These centres provide access to expertise, facilities, and potential funding opportunities. HVMC

This initiative offers funding to UK manufacturers for digital transformation, including the adoption of additive manufacturing technologies. It aims to enhance productivity and competitiveness. Made Smarter

Institutions like the Centre for Additive Manufacturing at the University of Nottingham host research grants from EPSRC, Innovate UK, and other funders, focusing on advancing additive manufacturing technologies. University of Nottingham

Stage 2 - Identify Applications

Identify Applications

Guide

Identify parts/products within your range that could benefit from AM. This includes evaluating the complexity of geometries that traditional manufacturing cannot easily achieve, the need for rapid prototyping or iterations, parts requiring high levels of customization, or low-volume production runs. Parts that need high precision, unique features, or specific mechanical properties (such as aerospace components or medical implants) are ideal candidates.

This stage involves mapping out a strategy for incorporating AM across different product lines and understanding the value AM can bring to each.

Case Studies

The team at Protostudios, a medical device prototyping lab, utilized Stratasys FDM and PolyJet technologies to rapidly develop and iterate on an improved version of the "Iowa Brace," a medical device for children with clubfoot. By choosing PC-ABS, a high-strength material, they produced 18 iterations efficiently, ensuring optimal design and functionality. This case exemplifies the importance of matching additive manufacturing technology and material properties to application needs, such as strength and customization for medical use.

In developing a marine hydrokinetic turbine blade, researchers evaluated multiple additive manufacturing (AM) processes and materials using a decision matrix. They determined that a hybrid approach combining Directed Energy Deposition (DED) for the structural box spar and FDM for the hydrodynamic fairings best met requirements for strength, environmental resistance, and cost efficiency. The process highlighted how AM technologies and compatible materials could be selected based on the component's functional and environmental demands.

Evaluation & Training

Guide

Additive manufacturing technologies like FDM, SLA, and SLS cater to different needs: FDM is affordable for thermoplastics, SLA offers precision with resins, and SLS excels with durable, complex parts. Materials include thermoplastics, metals, and composites, selected for properties like strength and flexibility. Choose in-house or outsourced printing based on scale and requirements.

Select printers based on speed, resolution, build volume, and material compatibility to meet production needs. Ensure they can handle complex designs reliably. Use software with features like DFAM optimization, error-checking, and CAD integration, along with tools for advanced slicing and machine diagnostics to enhance efficiency.

Train teams in printer operation, material handling, and DFAM to optimize designs and processes. Include troubleshooting and maintenance in training, and encourage continuous learning to stay updated on AM advancements and best practices.

Case Studies

FGAM workshops focused on training participants in advanced 3D printing techniques, such as voxel modeling, to optimize aerospace and automotive components. The impact was a deeper understanding of compatible technologies and material properties, leading to better-designed AM components. The takeaway is that specialized training enables more efficient use of advanced AM techniques, enhancing product design in high-performance sectors.

Siemens integrated additive manufacturing into its operations by training employees and redesigning parts for AM compatibility. This approach allowed for quicker adoption of AM for high-performance industrial components. The key takeaway is that investing in training accelerates the transition to AM, helping companies optimize production and achieve better component performance.

Stage 4 - Design for AM
▼
◀

Design for AM

Guide

One of the key advantages of AM is its ability to create complex, lightweight, and optimized parts. Redesign existing parts to leverage AM capabilities, such as reducing the number of components by consolidating them into a single 3D-printed part or using lattice structures to reduce weight while maintaining strength. Optimize parts for easier post-processing, enhance thermal performance, or allow for greater customization. A well-thought-out DFAM process can improve material efficiency and reduce production costs. Encourage collaboration between design, engineering, and production teams to achieve the best results.

Case Studies

Volkswagen used Design for Additive Manufacturing (DFAM) to redesign car door nozzles into a single Metal AM component, resulting in significant cost and time savings. This redesign reduced lead times from weeks to days and saved 650% in costs. The takeaway is that applying DFAM principles can lead to substantial improvements in cost-efficiency and production timelines.

GE Aviation applied DFAM to optimize turbine blades, improving their performance and reducing material waste. This redesign enhanced part reliability and production efficiency, crucial for the aerospace industry. The takeaway is that DFAM not only improves manufacturability but also enhances the performance of critical components, especially in sectors like aviation.

Quality Control & Testing

Guide

Implement rigorous quality control processes tailored to additive manufacturing. Since AM parts are often made with different materials and methods than traditional parts, new testing and inspection procedures must be considered.

Techniques like non-destructive testing (e.g., X-ray or ultrasonic testing), mechanical testing (e.g., tensile, fatigue, and impact testing), and surface finish evaluation are crucial to ensure product performance and quality. Maintain detailed records of testing results to monitor consistency across production runs and improve part reliability.

Case Studies

BMW implemented advanced quality control protocols, including non-destructive testing (NDT) methods like X-ray imaging, to ensure 3D-printed automotive parts met safety and performance standards. This process ensured the reliability of AM parts, essential for automotive safety. The takeaway is that robust quality control is vital to ensure AM parts meet rigorous safety and performance standards.

NASA used NDT to test the structural integrity of 3D-printed rocket engine parts under extreme conditions. This testing ensured the parts were reliable and could withstand the stresses of space missions. The takeaway is that thorough testing is critical for ensuring the reliability and safety of AM parts in high-risk, high-performance applications like aerospace.


Statistics

Implementing machine learning techniques in AM has led to significant improvements in defect detection. For instance, a study demonstrated a classification accuracy of approximately 95% when distinguishing between keyhole and lack of fusion defects, as well as process pores. PMC.gov

Research involving over 1,000 tensile tests on additively manufactured stainless steel revealed that rare defects, affecting about 2% of the population, caused a substantial loss in ductility. These defects were associated with an interconnected network of porosity, underscoring the need for rigorous quality control to detect even infrequent anomalies. OSTI.gov

Studies have shown that defect populations in AM materials possess features that can significantly impact fatigue and fracture properties. This variability highlights the necessity for robust statistical process control (SPC) methods to monitor and control the manufacturing process effectively. Case.edu

Advancements in predictive modeling for AM defects have shown a 43% improvement in predicting defects without losing precision. This enhancement aids in preemptively identifying potential quality issues, allowing for corrective actions before defects manifest in the final product. Taylor&Francis.com

Stage 6 - Integration
▼
◀

Integration

Guide

Additive manufacturing should be integrated into existing workflows, such as product design, engineering, and production. Align AM with your supply chain by working with procurement to identify material suppliers and ensure the efficient delivery of raw materials.

Collaboration across departments (e.g., R&D, quality control, and logistics) will ensure smooth transitions and help identify the best opportunities to optimize processes. Determine where AM will bring value, whether it's for rapid prototyping, tooling, or end-use production.

Case Studies

Honeywell integrated AM into its supply chain to produce critical components like heat exchangers, working closely with design, engineering, and procurement teams to streamline operations. The integration improved flexibility and reduced lead times. The takeaway is that cross-departmental collaboration is essential for successfully incorporating AM into existing supply chains.

Ford adopted a hybrid manufacturing process combining traditional and AM methods to produce custom tools and parts, improving flexibility and reducing lead times. This approach allowed Ford to leverage AM's benefits while maintaining existing production systems. The takeaway is that combining traditional manufacturing with AM can optimize existing workflows and enhance production efficiency.


Challenges

The initial investment in AM equipment, materials, and software can be substantial. Additionally, ongoing expenses for maintenance and training may pose financial challenges, particularly for small to medium-sized enterprises. Sybridge.com

A shortage of personnel with expertise in AM necessitates either training existing staff or recruiting skilled professionals, both of which require time and resources. Materialise.com

Aligning AM with current workflows and supply chains can be complex, often requiring process redesigns and ensuring compatibility between AM and traditional manufacturing methods. Materialise.com

Maintaining consistent quality in AM-produced parts is challenging due to process variability. Establishing robust quality control systems and adhering to industry standards are essential yet complex tasks. AMFG.ai

IP & Regulatory Compliance

Guide

As AM can enable the production of custom, often complex parts, intellectual property protection is a critical consideration. Ensure that your designs are protected by patents or copyrights where appropriate, and establish clear guidelines regarding who owns the rights to 3D printed designs and models.

Additionally, ensure that all your products comply with relevant regulatory standards, especially if you're working in regulated sectors like aerospace, automotive, or healthcare. This may include ensuring materials are certified for specific applications or that the final printed parts meet safety standards.

Case Studies

GE developed a 3D-printed fuel nozzle for jet engines, complying with aerospace industry standards. The company implemented rigorous testing and documentation to meet FAA regulations, highlighting the importance of compliance in regulated sectors like aviation.

Stryker, a medical device manufacturer, created 3D-printed titanium implants. The company adhered to medical device regulations by ensuring material traceability and quality control, demonstrating compliance in the healthcare sector.


Important Facts

The rise of 3D printing has introduced complexities in IP law, as traditional protections may not fully address the challenges posed by digital designs and easy replication. In the UK, design registrations offer exclusive rights to make, sell, and use a product embodying a registered design, providing a robust form of IP protection for 3D-printed products. TCT Magazine.com

In the medical sector, 3D-printed devices are subject to stringent regulatory frameworks. Manufacturers must adhere to specific standards, conduct necessary conformity assessments, and maintain comprehensive technical documentation to ensure safety and efficacy. The Medicines and Healthcare products Regulatory Agency (MHRA) provides guidance on the requirements for 3D-printed medical devices in the UK. GOV.uk

AM equipment must comply with established safety standards to protect operators and users. Guidelines are available to assist manufacturers in selecting appropriate safety standards and ensuring that equipment meets necessary safety requirements. For instance, the Low Voltage Directive (2014/35/EU) and the Machinery Directive (2006/42/EC) are relevant to AM equipment safety in the UK. Sourcing Speak.com

Cost Analysis & ROI

Guide

Carefully evaluate the costs associated with implementing and maintaining additive manufacturing technologies. This includes the upfront costs of purchasing equipment, ongoing material and labor costs, as well as the costs of software licenses and training. Analyze the ROI by comparing the costs of AM against traditional manufacturing processes in terms of production speed, flexibility, material waste, and quality. Monitor performance over time to ensure that your AM investment continues to provide value, and adjust your strategy as necessary based on operational results and changing business needs.

Case Studies

Ford implemented additive manufacturing for prototyping and small-batch production, analyzing costs related to equipment, materials, and labor. By focusing on cost-effective applications, such as jigs and fixtures, Ford achieved significant cost savings and reduced lead times.

Siemens integrated additive manufacturing for turbine components, conducting a detailed ROI analysis. The transition to 3D-printed parts improved performance and reduced production costs, demonstrating the financial viability of adopting additive manufacturing for critical components.
